Week 4: the GNU Manifesto

Richard Stallman, The GNU Manifesto

First of all, I feel that this manifesto is missing the point of its overall goal. By taking the path of acute obsession over what ‘free software’ means and doesn’t mean, it does what it strives to avoid; confuse the interested party. While the term ‘open source’ may not appeal with the patrons of GNU, it loses an important fact about marketing a movement; ease of inspiration. ‘Open source’ is currently welcoming, and easily understood. Rambling on with a diatribe about the different meanings of ‘free’ and the applicable context in this stadium loses traction while trying to mobilize the audience. Then again, when the term ‘open source’ was first utilized in its present context, they probably took multiple pages to explain it as well… (and in fact is the case as mentioned on the Philosophy page of the site, where it also mentions how the argument for the term “‘open source’ pays no attention to right and wrong, only to popularity and success…”).

As I continued reading, I came to understand the writer’s obsession with the semantics of ‘free software’. It is a necessary confusion. Mainly from the roots of developing the GNU system itself. Removing the shackles of capitalistic control means fully committing to the movement of fully accessible, public software. The fact that information accessibility and software development must be a public right. Hence, yes, ‘Free Software’ is the larger, more meaningful term than ‘open software’. I stand corrected.

About mid-way through the manifesto, one thing is clear; the goal is to promote the philosophy first and GNU is the embodiment of this philosophy. Whereas GNU may not be realistic for all companies, as they require the ‘person-power’ of tech support services, there should be an alternative for those who can take on that work themselves (or pay-as-you-go support services). At its core, it is more about the accessibility of software than the reach or promotion of GNU itself. This is emphasized in the manner that ‘marketing and distribution’ is discussed – ‘word of mouth’ is the preferrable marketing or PR tactic – and understandably so – in a not-for-profit system it not only spreads the product but relies on the method to build, bridge and sustain a community. For profit systems have to pay for this find of strength and longevity. To repurpose a sentiment in the manifesto, “When there is a deliberate choice to restrict, the harmful consequences are deliberate destruction”.

“When there is a deliberate choice to restrict, the harmful consequences are deliberate destruction”.

A thought comes to mind. Most of the programmers at the level that can create, manage and sustain software at this level are probably (quite) well paid in other facets of their career. Those doing this ‘free’ work on top of their personal economy are sharing the wealth in a societally productive manner – something that those in places of privilege should do in a society – build and sustain communities and accessibilities for all – to promote the equal advancement and sustainability of a community. The creative community promotes this often.

In the final paragraph the goal is clearer. Software development and redistribution could be a part of an integrated future that prioritizes people and accessibility over profit. And that, I support.

Week 3: Web Work: A History of Internet Art

and

The New World of Net Art

Rachel Greene, Web Work: A History of Internet Art, ArtForum, 2011

Carolina Miranda, The New World of Net Art

Internet (net.art) created a medium and a community to revive the art scene that imploded through the decimation by commercial exploitation and consumerism of pop art esthetics. It had become elite and controlled by the upper-yuppy-class. The gatekeepers with a strict members only-list. Exactly what the communities strive to destroy in the early years of any creative class.

The open format, accessibility and anonymity created a new spark for creativity and the ‘methods, production and distribution’ allowed by the new art form became the stamp and the aesthetic of the new media model. JoDI.org’s work is intriguing and engaging in the ways in which it ‘stumps even the most computer literate’. The idea that you are constantly questioning, “What for and why?’ is such an integral but simple question that, really should be injected into many more artforms than it is (and it is). The catch phrase personifying the existential dread of society and culture through a piece of interactive web art.

I was introduced to the VNS Matrix and the cyberfeminism movement that included the Australian collective a couple of years ago when researching new forms of feminist art. It is a concept that connects with me closely because of their ideas of the power in the genderless connected with feminism and the ways in which this approach to gender scrambles the ‘mind of the BIG DADDY MAINFRAME’. It. Does. Not. COM.PUTE> It is the power in the cunt – the embodied and the quintessence. I interpreter this as the cunt can be a female, trans or a genderless’s embodied power. Much of this is connected to the Cyborg Manifesto by Donna Haraway – the dismissal of the shackles that separate human and machine and human and animal. The strength in the unification of these entities and what they mean in the technological overworld.

'The strength in the unification of these entities and what they mean in the technological overworld.'

Moving to the article, The New World Of Net.Art , I am inspired by Christiane Paul’s comment of how NetArt has evolved to take on different manifestations, moving from the virtual world, to standalone computer applications on to out-of-the-box, IRL installations. It is something that I am striving to embody in my own creative practice. The arm that reaches out from the virtual world to connect with the humanity of the physical world. Funnily enough, I too have, ‘a bit of that ADD mentality’.

While reading about Jon Rafman being included in this article, it was an interesting thought that the very medium that made this web-artist famous brought him crashing down – he has been accused of sexual misconduct by a number of women in the Montreal community, and as of the past year or so has been dropped by some of his gallery representatives. Highlighting one of the strengths of this medium as opposed to others – there is now nowhere to hide your abuse in the circuits of the internet. The community of whispers that used to protect many women in a creative community is now screamed from the platforms of the digital realm. It is poetic to think that the world from where his success was birthed also tore him down.

Week 2: The Time Canada Wanted Its Own Internet Because It Thought the US Would Mess it Up

and

From Counterculture to Cyberculture

Daniel Joseph, The Time Canada Wanted Its Own Internet..., VICE, July 13, 2018

Fred Turner, From Counterculture to Cyberculture, Chapter 4, Taking the Whole Earth Digital

‘The Time Canada Wanted Its Own Internet Because It Thought the US would Mess it Up’ -and rightly so. As we know now, there is an onslaught of dangerous misinformation and corporate monopolization of information on the mainly American-owned World Wide Web of today - although, to be fair, any for profit system spawned from the recesses of capitalistic greed would produce a close replication, no matter how ‘politely Canadian’ it may be. However, it is interesting to muse about another alternate reality where Canada has their own internet, and how those distinctly non-Americanisms of the Canadian ideations may have produced a slightly different network of media and open form information. It’s nice to think that a Canadian government run internet may not sell the information of its people to corporations, while using the behavioral and psychological intersections of addiction to fuse our brains to the social media mutant that plagues much of society today. I have some faith in Canada but maybe not that much.

As for Counterculture to Cyberculture, the imagery of the computer nerds crossing over with the hippies and the ‘freaks’ - as so many people from that era called themselves if they were the outsiders, but not necessarily hippies- is not a foreign concept to me as it is similar to the history of electroacoustic and early electronic music (I am majoring in Electroacoustic studies). While the computer systems of those days lived exclusively in the basements of the higher education -where ‘cyber-futurists’ would interact with complex systems while contemplating the positive impact it could have on art and culture - the same was happening with giant synthesizers and oscillators ‘next door’. There was always an underground approach to ‘higher technologies’ throughout the ages. Even though, to the majority of the public, computers and technology was originally thought of for the elite, in actuality it was the counterculturalists who were the first to manifest the push for the open access information-super highway that was made possible by the internet. It’s ingrained into its history, and thus branded in every circuit.

‘You don't have to leave industrial society, but you don't have to accept it the way it is…’. This quote from From Counterculture to Cyberculture rings particularly true to me right now, with the very polarized group of radicalized friends; some wishing to leave society and farm off-the-grid while others argue the importance to stay engaged in society and fight for change. The difficulty of industry and technology in the past and present is the control that capitalism has on weaponizing those bodies for their needs of profit. This huge burdensome cloud over the positive advancements that technology and computers have afforded many people - more particularly to those who may not have otherwise been afforded the luxury of information and world-wide reaching discourse between different communities (referring to the ways in which social media has connected people, and empowered positive change, while putting the power of free education back into the hands of the people). The essence of ‘open source’ as a countercultural concept cannot be understated. Although the ideals prophesied in the early internet age have been diluted and over taken by the darkness of mis-information and lack of trust in the media - and even science its self - there still lives the power for many avenues to promote shared education and technological opportunities that may not have been afforded if those ideals were not present in the early computer days.

'The difficulty of industry and technology in the past and present is the control that capitalism has on weaponizing those bodies for their needs of profit.'

Week 1: As We May Think

As We May Think, The Atlantic, July 1945

It is always to entertaining read old writings that muse and hypothesise future technology – even more enlightening when some points made are (seemly) accidentally, but right on the money. The bulk of the paper explores the current and near future (of that time) status of data and the handling and processing of it. The way it is the heart of technology and progressing human research. Paragraph after paragraph unravelling what is, essentially at the core of information technology. It was always about the recording, storage, organization, transmission, analysis and communication of data. The heart of computer science. There are so many instances where the author is close to embodying a technological Nostradamus -almost- with the ‘memex’ recalling a type of early iPhone prototype.

The piece is taken from Atlantic’s 2018’s collection of iconic articles over the 160 years of the Atlantic’s publication. It is definitely an important and predictive piece of writing produced at such an iconic moment in time. After the second world war came to a close and all of the death and destruction, helped largely by the technological advancements of the time, paused for a moment. The author contemplates a peaceful wish in the first paragraph… The hope that the next generation of technological advancement is towards the betterment of the human race – and there has been aspects of that. However, much like the original purpose of many technologies at the time that were then weaponized, data sciences and computer sciences have also seen their usefulness weaponized and wielded to pit human against human. The simple bases of that technology - to organize, store and transmit data in the quickest, most effective and efficient methods possible have breed both the best and the worst in society.
